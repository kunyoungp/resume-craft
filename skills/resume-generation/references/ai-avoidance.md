# AI-Avoidance Guide

Make every piece of generated content sound authentically human. AI-detection tools and experienced recruiters both flag the same patterns — generic phrasing, uniform sentence structure, and corporate buzzwords. Follow every rule below to produce writing that reads as if a real person wrote it.

---

## Banned Word/Phrase List

Flag and replace every instance of these words and phrases. No exceptions.

| Banned | Replace with |
|---|---|
| Leverage / leveraging | Use, apply, build on |
| Utilize / utilized | Use, used |
| Delve / delving | Explore, examine, dig into |
| Comprehensive | Thorough, complete, full |
| Holistic | Complete, whole, integrated |
| Innovative solutions | [describe the actual solution] |
| Results-driven | [show results instead of claiming to be driven by them] |
| Data-driven | [show data usage instead] |
| Actionable insights | Findings, recommendations, takeaways |
| Streamline / streamlined | Simplified, cut steps from, shortened |
| Nuanced | Detailed, specific, subtle |
| Pivotal | Key, important, central |
| Crucial / vital | Important, key, necessary |
| Foster / fostering | Build, grow, encourage, support |
| Ensure / ensuring | Make sure, confirm, verify |
| Furthermore / moreover | Also, and, plus — or just start a new sentence |
| Track record of success | [cite specific successes instead] |
| Strong collaborator | [describe a collaboration instead] |
| Ever-evolving landscape | [name the specific change] |
| Spearheaded | Led, started, launched, drove |
| Synergy / synergies | Cooperation, combined effort, partnership |
| Paradigm shift | Change, transformation |
| Best practices | Standards, proven methods |
| Cutting-edge | New, advanced, latest |
| Robust | Strong, solid, reliable |

When replacing a banned word, do not simply swap in another generic term. Rewrite the entire sentence to be specific. For example:

- BAD: "Leveraged data-driven insights to streamline operations"
- STILL BAD: "Used data insights to simplify operations"
- GOOD: "Analyzed 6 months of warehouse throughput data and cut the pick-pack-ship cycle from 4 steps to 2, saving 12 hours per week"

The goal is not synonym replacement — it is specificity. Every rewrite should add a concrete detail that was missing from the original.

## Structural Checks

### 1. Sentence Length Variation

Calculate the word count of every sentence in a paragraph or section. Flag any sequence where more than 3 consecutive sentences have word counts within plus or minus 3 words of each other.

Good writing mixes short punchy sentences (5-8 words) with longer detailed ones (15-25 words). Monotonous rhythm is the single strongest AI signal in written text.

Example of BAD rhythm (all sentences ~12-14 words):
- "I managed a team of engineers to build the new payment platform." (13 words)
- "I coordinated with product managers to define the project requirements clearly." (11 words)
- "I implemented automated testing to reduce the number of production bugs." (11 words)
- "I delivered the project ahead of schedule and under the planned budget." (12 words)

Example of GOOD rhythm:
- "Managed a team of 6 engineers." (6 words)
- "We rebuilt the payment platform from scratch — new architecture, new CI pipeline, new monitoring stack — and shipped it 3 weeks early." (22 words)
- "Bug reports dropped 40%." (4 words)
- "The PM team said it was the smoothest launch they'd seen in two years, which I took as the highest compliment." (21 words)

### 2. No "Rule of Three" in Consecutive Bullets

The pattern "Did X, Y, and Z" is a classic AI writing tell when it appears in more than 2 consecutive bullet points. If you find this pattern in 3 or more consecutive bullets, rewrite at least one to break the pattern.

- BAD (three consecutive "rule of three" bullets):
  - "Designed, developed, and deployed the customer portal"
  - "Managed, mentored, and motivated a team of 8"
  - "Researched, proposed, and implemented a new CI/CD pipeline"

- GOOD (varied structures):
  - "Built the customer portal from design through deployment — React frontend, Node backend, AWS hosting"
  - "Managed a team of 8 engineers. Ran weekly 1:1s and quarterly growth reviews."
  - "Replaced the legacy Jenkins pipeline with GitHub Actions, cutting build times from 25 minutes to 7"

### 3. Use Contractions Naturally

Mix contractions with formal phrasing. Do not use contractions in every sentence, and do not avoid them entirely. Real people naturally alternate. In a 300-word cover letter, use at least 3-5 contractions.

- Use "I've built" alongside "I have experience"
- Use "don't" alongside "do not"
- Use "it's" alongside "it is"

The key is natural mixing. All contractions sounds too casual. Zero contractions sounds robotic.

### 4. Specific Opening Lines

Every opening line — whether a resume summary, cover letter intro, or section header — must be specific, not abstract.

- BAD: "I am passionate about leveraging technology to drive innovation."
- BAD: "Dedicated professional with a proven track record of success."
- BAD: "Experienced engineer seeking new opportunities in a dynamic environment."
- GOOD: "I rebuilt our checkout flow last quarter, cutting cart abandonment by 23%."
- GOOD: "Backend engineer, 6 years in distributed systems, mostly Go and Python."
- GOOD: "Your team's work on real-time fraud detection is exactly what I've spent the last 3 years building at FinCo."

The test: does the opening line contain a specific fact (a number, a technology, a company name, a project)? If not, rewrite it.

### 5. Start Some Sentences with "And" or "But"

Humans do this naturally. AI tends not to. In every cover letter, include at least 1-2 sentences starting with "And" or "But."

- "And that experience taught me more about scaling systems than any textbook."
- "But the real win was the 15% improvement in user retention we saw three months later."

Do not overuse this — once or twice per document is enough.

### 6. Verifiable Details in Every Claim

Every single claim in the resume or cover letter must include at least one verifiable detail: a number, a date, a project name, a company, a tool, a specific outcome. If a bullet point has no verifiable detail, it is incomplete.

- BAD: "Improved system performance"
- GOOD: "Improved API response times from 800ms to 120ms by adding Redis caching and query optimization"

## The "Read Aloud Test"

Read every sentence aloud — or mentally simulate reading it aloud. Apply these checks:

1. **Does it sound like a press release?** If yes, rewrite. Press releases use passive voice, abstract nouns, and corporate jargon.
2. **Does it sound like a corporate memo?** If yes, rewrite. Memos use "ensure alignment," "drive initiatives," and "leverage synergies."
3. **Does it sound like a college admissions essay?** If yes, rewrite. Admissions essays use "passionate about," "unique perspective," and "diverse experiences."
4. **Would you say this in a job interview?** If not, rewrite. Interview language is direct, specific, and conversational.

The target voice is: a competent professional explaining their work to a colleague over coffee. Not formal. Not casual. Just clear and specific.
